Paryushana is one of the most important Jain festivals, emphasizing spiritual renewal through fasting, forgiveness, introspection, and self-discipline, usually celebrated in August or September. Meaning and Significance Paryushana, meaning "abiding and coming together," is a sacred Jain festival spanning 8 days for Śvētāmbara Jains and 10 days for Digambara Jains. It is a time for devotees to pause worldly activities and deepen their spiritual practice through fasting, prayer, meditation, and introspection. The festival encourages the principles of truth, non-violence, forgiveness, and renunciation, aiming for purification of mind and soul. The final day celebrates Samvatsari, a day dedicated to seeking forgiveness from others by saying "Micchami Dukkadam," which means, "If I have offended you in any way, knowingly or unknowingly, in thought, word, or deed, then I seek your forgiveness" Wikipedia +2 . Dates for 2025 In 2025, Paryushana is observed from August 21 to August 28 by the Śvētāmbara community, concluding with Samvatsari on August 28. The Digambara sect begins the festival a day later, observing a 10-day period focused on ten essential dharmas including truth and humility Wikipedia +1 . Rituals and Observances Fasting: Many Jains fast during Paryushana, which can range from partial fasting to consuming only boiled water between sunrise and sunset. The duration of fasting varies per individual's capacity, lasting from a day to several days or even longer 2 . Pratikraman: A ritual of introspection and repentance where followers examine their actions and seek forgiveness from both the divine and other people. Temple Processions & Sacred Texts: Devotees attend Jain temples, participate in processions, and listen to or recite scriptures such as the Kalpa Sūtra. Meditation and Mantras: Focused meditation and chanting of mantras, especially the teachings of Lord Mahavir, help purify the mind. Forgiveness: On the final day or Samvatsari, followers request forgiveness to clear any ill feelings from the past year 2 . 2 Sources Food and Dietary Practices Jain dietary customs during Paryushana reflect Ahimsa (non-violence), with strict avoidance of root vegetables (like onions, garlic, potatoes, carrots), fermented foods, and sometimes leafy greens to minimize harm to living organisms in plants and soil. Meals are consumed before sunset to avoid killing nocturnal insects. Many follow a sattvic (pure) diet with simple, light foods such as sabudana khichdi, buckwheat pancakes, dal dhokli, lauki soup, and fruit-based dishes. Modern adaptations replace potatoes with pumpkin or raw bananas and use alternative seasonings like hing (asafoetida) instead of garlic India TV News +1 . Cultural Impact In parts of India with large Jain populations, such as Maharashtra, Gujarat, and Rajasthan, slaughterhouses often close during Paryushana as a mark of respect for the festival and the Jain principle of non-violence Wikipedia . Summary Paryushana is a profound period of forgiveness, fasting, meditation, and spiritual renewal for Jains worldwide. It encourages reflection on one's actions, strict adherence to non-violence, and seeking peace within and with others, culminating in the ritualistic request for forgiveness that strengthens community bonds and inner purity Wikipedia +2 . If you observe Paryushana in Pune or anywhere in India, it is a time to focus on peace, purity, and spiritual growth through these age-old Jain traditions.
